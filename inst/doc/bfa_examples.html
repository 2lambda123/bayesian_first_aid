binom.test

A classical binomial test:

binom.test(x = 16, n = 20)

## 
##  Exact binomial test
## 
## data:  16 and 20
## number of successes = 16, number of trials = 20, p-value = 0.01182
## alternative hypothesis: true probability of success is not equal to 0.5
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  0.5634 0.9427
## sample estimates:
## probability of success 
##                    0.8

Preprend binom.test by bfa. to run the Bayesian “version”.

bfa.binom.test(x = 16, n = 20)

## 
##  Bayesian first aid binomial test
## 
## data: 16 and 20
## number of successes = 16, number of trials = 20
## Estimated relative frequency of success:
##   0.772 
## 95 percent credible interval:
##   0.601 0.928 
## The relative frequency of success is more than 0.5 by a probability of 0.996 
## and less than 0.5 by a probability of 0.004

The idea is to respect the arguments to the original binom.test as far as possible. For example, setting p and alternative as in…

binom.test(x = 16, n = 20, p = 0.75, conf.level = 0.8)

## 
##  Exact binomial test
## 
## data:  16 and 20
## number of successes = 16, number of trials = 20, p-value = 0.7977
## alternative hypothesis: true probability of success is not equal to 0.75
## 80 percent confidence interval:
##  0.6393 0.9098
## sample estimates:
## probability of success 
##                    0.8

… is respected by bfa.binom.test:

bfa.binom.test(x = 16, n = 20, p = 0.75, conf.level = 0.8)

## 
##  Bayesian first aid binomial test
## 
## data: 16 and 20
## number of successes = 16, number of trials = 20
## Estimated relative frequency of success:
##   0.772 
## 80 percent credible interval:
##   0.67 0.885 
## The relative frequency of success is more than 0.75 by a probability of 0.629 
## and less than 0.75 by a probability of 0.371

By assigning the result of bfa.binom.test to a variable we can get some more usefull info by calling plot for looking at the posterior (and perhaps a posterior predictive check), summary for a more extensive summary than the standard print out above, and diagnostics for MCMC diagnostics. The idea is that the behavior of these functions should be consistent for the output of all the bfa.*.test functions. 

fit <- bfa.binom.test(x = 16, n = 20)
plot(fit)

t.test

The alternative for the t.test is of course BEST.

x <- rnorm(n = 30, mean = 10, sd = 5)
t.test(x)

## 
##  One Sample t-test
## 
## data:  x
## t = 15.72, df = 29, p-value = 1.001e-15
## alternative hypothesis: true mean is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##   9.899 12.860
## sample estimates:
## mean of x 
##     11.38

bfa.t.test(x)

## 
##  Bayesian estimation supersedes the t test (BEST)
## 
## data: x
## 
##   Estimates [95 percent credible interval]
## mean of x: 11.22 [9.627, 12.71]
## sd of x: 3.868 [2.727, 5.104]
## 
## The mean is more than 0 by a probability of 0.999 
## and less than 0 by a probability of 0.001
